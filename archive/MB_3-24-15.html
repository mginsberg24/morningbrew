MARKET MINUTE: EARNINGS SEASON ON THE HORIZON

The market appears to be walking on thin ice, as Wall Street is looking past this week’s performance to the start of earnings season next week. The indices all sustained minor drops, the Nasdaq suffering the most due to heavy losses in the biotechnology sector. The indices may sail through some rough waters this week as they cope with companies releasing premature earnings, many of which will be affected by the recent depreciation of foreign currencies and lower oil prices.

WORLD MACRO: EUROZONE UPDATE

The Euro

What’s happening to the Euro? Lets use an analogy: owning a Euro is like owning a share in a company. When that company shows a sign of weakness, the investor will sell his share. Last week, the Euro hit a 12-year low of $1.05 a Euro, suggesting that there were more sellers than buyers of the euro, thus creating excess capital outflow. Yesterday, the Euro picked itself up and added 1.2% against the greenback to close at $1.0927.

The ECB's Role

Many worry that Europeans have turned to the European Central Bank (ECB) for assistance. ECB President Mario Draghi has supported a $1.3 trillion quantitative easing (QE) program, where the ECB buys up bonds to improve liquidity in European countries, hopefully stimulating the economy. As for recent controversy surrounding Greece, $105B has already been lent and Greece wants more. Neighboring nations feel that Greece is blackmailing the ECB for funds. That is, if Greece dies, it will drag the EU down with it - down to the depths of economic Hades.

U.S. MACRO: TAKE EQUITIES WITH EASE 

The Bull

Equities continue to post positive returns as they confidently saunter into a highly anticipated earnings season. The Fed’s easy-breezy policy and European quantitative easing tactics have relaxed the concerns of many over the past few weeks. These days, investors everywhere have a spring in their step. Given attractive returns coupled with historically low bond yields, some money managers feel entry into the market is as attractive as it has ever been.

The Bear

Despite rejoicing choirs of investors, doubts and troubles that once weighed heavy still exist. Oil’s continued struggles will be sure to hit the energy sector pretty hard, and the appreciation of the U.S. dollar against other currencies will be sure to hurt multinationals operating and exporting abroad. Finally, expensive U.S. equities may become a barrier to entry, encouraging investors to seek growth and value in cheaper international markets instead.

SECTOR SPECIFIC: MARGINAL RISE IN EXISTING HOME SALES

Neighborhood Watch: U.S. existing home sales in February rose a measly 1.2%, following a 4.9% dip in January. Existing home sales, which account for the majority of home purchases, historically tend to rise 6% this time of year (springtime, that is). The likely culprit for these weak figures is a supply shortage. With a paltry 1.89 million homes for sale on the market, the median price of homes has risen, sales growth has slowed, and buyers are realizing that maybe their dream homes aren’t somewhere out there after all.

STUDENT LOANS: CRISIS OR RESOLUTION?

The Business Side

The student loan crisis has haunted the US economy for years. With $1.2 trillion in student loans, high default rates, and average debt rising every year, a solution has to be in our near future, right? Right, guys? Anyone? Entrepreneur and billionaire Mark Cuban foretells a grisly future, a future eerily similar to the collapse of the housing bubble. With no current cap on borrowing, student loans practically come free with happy meals, and colleges are taking full advantage of the “free” money by boosting prices (tuition rates, textbooks, etc). When the government eventually caps borrowing, which shaman Cuban insists is in our near future, the price structure will dissipate, overburdening students.

Student Loans by the Numbers

  • The number of Americans with student loans is greater than the entire populations of Canada, Poland, North Korea, Australia, and more than 200 other countries.
  • With seven million Americans defaulting on their student loans, this also means that seven million Americans will have their personal credit plummet and be deemed unemployable by many employers.
  • If you default on your student loans, you can be stripped of your professional license and even have your driver's license suspended as punishment.
  • The Congressional Budget Office estimates that the Federal Government made about $50B on student loans in 2013.

TODAY IN TECH: SHOULD WE SHUT OFF LOCATION SERVICES?

A Carnegie Mellon University study concluded that a handful of the most popular smartphone apps collect location data around 3,100 times per week. Although it would seem this knowledge benefits the apps themselves, the truth is more sinister. It turns out that 73% of the time, the collected information is being shared with advertising networks, who care about your whereabouts even more than your mom does. Marketers find it more effective to run the advertisement of a store or product when it is nearby the user, and are willing to pay a beefy 10% to 20% premium for ads that include location information. No wonder Groupon needs your exact location every 3 minutes.

TODAY IN TECH: MUSIC INDUSTRY NOT SINGING PRAISES OF INTERNET

Internet music providers such as Spotify and Pandora have gained serious traction as of late, but big record companies are starting to kill their vibes. Companies such as Sony and Universal are trying to leverage their positions as content creators to force changes on the internet streamers, such as an end to free streaming. Don’t lament just yet - many believe online streaming to be the legitimate future of the music industry, a better alternative to CDs and digital downloads. Online streamers and their disciples are not going away without a fight, and record companies may soon learn that it is better to cooperate than fight and lose out.
